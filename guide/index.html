OpenClaw for Beginners

If you’ve heard the buzz about OpenClaw but aren’t quite sure what it is or why people are excited, this section is for you. No technical background required.

What is OpenClaw?

OpenClaw is a free, open-source program that turns AI models like Claude, ChatGPT, or open-source alternatives into a personal assistant that can actually do things on your behalf — not just answer questions.

Think of it this way: ChatGPT is like having a very smart friend you can text for advice. OpenClaw is like hiring that smart friend as your full-time personal assistant, giving them the keys to your house, your email password, and your calendar, and saying “handle it.”

It was created by Peter Steinberger and released in early 2026. In just 60 days it became the fastest-growing open-source project in GitHub’s history, reaching 247,000+ stars — a measure of popularity among developers.

How Does It Work?

OpenClaw runs as a program on a computer you control — your laptop, a home server, or a cheap cloud server you rent. It connects two things together:

  1. AI “brains” — Large language models (LLMs) like Claude or GPT that understand your requests and figure out what to do
  2. Your digital life — Your email, calendar, messaging apps, smart home devices, files, and more

You talk to it through apps you already use — Signal, Telegram, WhatsApp, or Discord. You send it a message like you’d text a friend, and it goes off and does things for you.

An analogy: Imagine a universal remote control, but instead of controlling just your TV, it can control your email, calendar, smart lights, music, and dozens of other things. And instead of pressing buttons, you just tell it what you want in plain English. That’s OpenClaw.

What Can It Actually Do?

The possibilities are broad because OpenClaw uses a skills system — modular add-ons that teach it how to interact with different services. Think of skills like apps on a smartphone: each one adds a new capability. A few quick examples:

  • Email assistant: Reads your inbox every morning, summarizes what’s important, and sends you a digest on Telegram before you even open your laptop
  • Smart home controller: “Turn off the downstairs lights and set the thermostat to 68” — via a text message from the couch
  • Research helper: “Find me the best-reviewed hiking boots under $200 and compare the top three”
  • Travel planner: Give it your dates and interests and it builds a full day-by-day itinerary
  • Cooking assistant: “What can I make with chicken thighs, rice, and whatever’s in season right now?”

The key difference from existing AI chatbots: OpenClaw doesn’t just tell you what to do. It does it. When you say “reschedule my 2pm to Thursday,” it actually moves the meeting on your calendar.

Another analogy: ChatGPT is like calling a brilliant advisor on the phone — they can give you ideas, but they can’t physically do anything for you. OpenClaw is like that same advisor sitting at your desk with access to your computer, your phone, and your filing cabinet. Much more powerful — but you need to think about what access you give them.

Why Is Everyone So Excited?

1. It’s the first AI agent that “just works” for everyday tasks. There have been AI agent projects before, but they were research demos or required deep technical expertise. OpenClaw has an onboarding wizard and a skills marketplace (ClawHub) that make it much more accessible — Steinberger’s goal is “an agent even my mum can use.”

2. You own your data. Unlike cloud AI services, OpenClaw runs on your hardware. Pair it with open-source AI models that run entirely on your machine, and your emails, health data, and personal notes never leave your network. In an era of growing privacy concerns, this is a big deal.

3. It works with any AI model. Not locked into one company. Want to use Claude for complex reasoning, a free open-source model for simple tasks, and a local model for anything sensitive? OpenClaw lets you mix and match.

4. It meets you where you are. Instead of learning a new app, you talk to it through messaging apps you already use every day. It fits into your life rather than demanding you change it.

5. It’s genuinely useful today. This isn’t a demo or a promise. People are using OpenClaw right now to manage their homes, track their health, plan trips, and handle dozens of daily tasks. Many users report getting back 1–3 hours per day.

What’s the Catch?

Security is on you. When you give an AI agent access to your email, calendar, and smart home, a security breach could mean someone reading your messages or unlocking your door. OpenClaw has had over 60 security vulnerabilities in its first four months. You need to keep it updated and properly configured.

The skills marketplace has a trust problem. ClawHub, where users share skills, was found to have roughly 1 in 5 skills containing malicious code in early 2026. You need to be careful about what you install.

It requires some technical comfort. As one community member put it: “If you can’t understand how to run a command line, this is far too dangerous of a project for you to use safely.”

It can make mistakes with real consequences. Unlike a chatbot where a wrong answer is just a wrong answer, an AI agent that takes actions can make real mistakes — sending an email to the wrong person, deleting a calendar event, or turning off your alarm system. Human oversight is essential, especially early on.

The bottom line OpenClaw is a genuine leap in what AI can do for you personally — it’s the difference between asking for directions and having a chauffeur. The next section covers 20 specific things you can do with it. Later sections cover how to set it up safely.

20 Things You Can Do with OpenClaw

Each use case starts with what’s possible, then flags the risks and how to handle them. Sorted roughly from everyday essentials to more creative uses.

1 Morning Briefing & Email Digest

HIGH RISK 30–45 min/day saved

Every morning before you wake up, OpenClaw reads your inbox, filters out the noise, and sends you a prioritized summary on Telegram or Signal. “Two emails need replies today, three packages are shipping, your dentist appointment is confirmed.” You start your day knowing exactly what matters.

Watch out for
  • Your email contents travel to an external AI for processing — that’s sensitive data leaving your machine
  • Spam or phishing emails could contain hidden instructions that trick the agent
Stay safe
  • Use a read-only email token — the agent can read but never send or delete
  • Filter out unknown senders before processing
  • Consider a local AI model to keep email data on your machine

2 Calendar & Scheduling

MEDIUM RISK 15–30 min/day saved

“Find a free hour this week for coffee with Alex.” OpenClaw checks your calendar, considers your preferences (no meetings before 10am, prefer afternoons for social), and proposes times. It can also give you a daily agenda briefing and warn about conflicts.

Watch out for
  • Calendar data reveals your location, habits, and social circle
  • Write access means it could accidentally delete or move meetings
Stay safe
  • Start with read-only access — let it suggest, but you confirm
  • Require approval before any changes to your calendar

3 Smart Home Control

HIGH RISK Convenience, 15–30 min/day

Control your entire home from a text message. “I’m heading to bed” — and the lights dim, the thermostat drops, the front porch light turns on. Or text from work: “Turn on the AC, I’ll be home in 20 minutes.” It can also monitor things: “Let me know if the garage door has been open for more than 30 minutes.”

Watch out for
  • Physical security implications — a compromised agent could unlock doors or disable cameras
  • Hidden instructions in other messages could trigger physical actions
Stay safe
  • Never connect locks or security systems — stick to lights, thermostats, speakers
  • Require a PIN or confirmation for anything security-related
  • Use a separate smart home hub as a buffer layer

4 Personal Finance & Budget Tracking

VERY HIGH RISK 2–3 hrs/week saved

OpenClaw connects to your bank via read-only APIs, categorizes every transaction, and sends you a weekly spending report. “You spent $340 on dining out this week, which is 40% over your usual.” It can track subscriptions, flag unusual charges, and help you stick to a budget — all without opening a spreadsheet.

Watch out for
  • Financial data is extremely sensitive — a breach exposes your entire spending history
  • Data sent to a cloud AI could be logged or leaked
Stay safe
  • Use read-only bank access (Plaid with read-only scope) — never grant transfer capabilities
  • Use a local AI model — do not send financial data to cloud APIs
  • Export transactions as CSV and process offline rather than live connections

5 Health & Fitness Tracking

HIGH RISK 30–60 min/day saved

Connect your Apple Watch, Fitbit, or Garmin data and get meaningful insights instead of raw numbers. “Your resting heart rate has trended up 5 bpm over the past two weeks — you might be overtraining or under-recovering.” It can track habits, correlate sleep quality with exercise, and generate weekly wellness summaries.

Watch out for
  • Health data is among the most sensitive categories (HIPAA, GDPR special category)
  • Incorrect health suggestions could be harmful
  • Health data sent to cloud AI could be used for insurance profiling
Stay safe
  • Use a local AI model exclusively — never send health data to cloud APIs
  • Never act on health suggestions without consulting a real doctor
  • Use read-only access to health platforms

6 Recipe & Meal Planning

LOW RISK 3–5 hrs/week saved

“Plan dinners for the week — we’re trying to eat more Mediterranean, and Tuesday is leftover night.” OpenClaw builds a meal plan, generates a consolidated grocery list (grouped by store aisle), and can even adjust portions based on who’s eating. Photograph your fridge and ask “what can I make with this?” for instant recipe ideas based on what you actually have.

Watch out for
  • Food allergy info is health-adjacent — incorrect handling could be dangerous
  • If connected to a grocery delivery service, write access means real purchases
Stay safe
  • Always double-check allergy and dietary restriction handling manually
  • Use draft/review mode for any grocery orders before they’re placed

7 Travel Planning & Itineraries

LOW RISK 5–10 hrs/trip saved

“I have 5 days in Tokyo in April. I love street food, architecture, and quiet neighborhoods. I don’t want anything too touristy.” OpenClaw builds a day-by-day itinerary with restaurant recommendations, transit directions, and backup rainy-day options. It can monitor flight prices, track your loyalty points, and even learn your travel preferences over time.

Watch out for
  • Travel dates and destinations reveal your location and absence from home
  • Recommendations could be hallucinated — a restaurant that doesn’t exist or closed last year
Stay safe
  • Verify key recommendations (restaurants, hours, addresses) before relying on them
  • Don’t share exact travel dates broadly — keep the agent’s memory private

8 Personal Knowledge Base / Second Brain

LOW RISK 1–2 hrs/day saved

Every article you save, note you jot down, or podcast highlight you capture gets automatically organized into a searchable personal library. Later, you can ask “what was that article about sleep and blue light I read last month?” and get the exact reference with your highlights. It connects ideas across sources and surfaces relevant old notes when you’re working on something new.

Watch out for
  • Personal notes sent to cloud AI for processing
  • Memory poisoning — malicious content could be injected into your knowledge base
Stay safe
  • Use a local AI model for processing personal notes
  • Periodically review for suspicious or unexpected content
  • Keep original sources alongside AI summaries

9 Workout Programming & Training Coach

LOW-MEDIUM RISK 3–5 hrs/week saved

Tell it your goals (“run a sub-4-hour marathon by October” or “get stronger without a gym”) and it builds a periodized training plan. It adapts based on how you feel: “I’m really sore today” adjusts the workout to active recovery. It can pull data from your watch to auto-adjust intensity, track PRs, and celebrate milestones.

Watch out for
  • Training advice without medical context could cause injury
  • Fitness data is health-sensitive
Stay safe
  • Use a local model for fitness data processing
  • Don’t follow AI training advice for rehab or injury recovery — see a professional

10 Research & Deep Dives

LOW-MEDIUM RISK 1–2 hrs/day saved

“I’m thinking about getting solar panels. Research the economics for a 2,000 sq ft house in Northern California — costs, savings, best brands, common pitfalls.” OpenClaw scours the web, compiles findings, and delivers a structured briefing with sources. Perfect for big purchase decisions, learning new topics, or staying current on hobbies and interests.

Watch out for
  • Websites can contain hidden instructions that hijack the agent’s browsing
  • Summaries may present hallucinations as facts
Stay safe
  • Run the browser in a sandboxed container
  • Cross-reference important findings manually before making decisions
  • Restrict browsing to trusted domains when possible

11 Language Learning & Translation

LOW RISK 2–4 hrs/week saved

Learning Japanese? OpenClaw becomes a patient, always-available conversation partner. It corrects your grammar in real time, explains nuances, creates custom flashcard decks from your mistakes, and adjusts difficulty as you improve. Traveling abroad? Send it a photo of a menu or sign and get instant translation with cultural context: “This dish is a local specialty — it’s spicy fermented tofu, very strong flavor.”

Watch out for
  • Mistranslations in medical or legal contexts could have real consequences
Stay safe
  • Don’t rely on it for critical translations (medical symptoms, legal documents)
  • Have a native speaker verify anything important

12 Family Coordination

MEDIUM RISK 30–60 min/day saved

The household command center. “What’s everyone doing this Saturday?” pulls from multiple family calendars. It tracks who needs to be where, sends reminders (“Soccer practice in 30 minutes — don’t forget cleats”), manages the family grocery list, and coordinates pickups. For shared households, it can split bills, track chores, and settle the eternal “whose turn is it?” debate.

Watch out for
  • Children’s schedule data is especially sensitive (COPPA implications)
  • Multiple people’s calendars means a broader exposure surface
Stay safe
  • Use read-only calendar access for family members
  • Keep children’s data processed locally, never through cloud AI
  • Require confirmation before sending messages on anyone’s behalf

13 Home Maintenance & Repair Tracker

LOW RISK 2–3 hrs/month saved

OpenClaw tracks everything about your home: when the furnace filter was last changed, the roof warranty expiration, the paint color in the guest bedroom (Benjamin Moore “Simply White” OC-117). It sends seasonal reminders (“Time to flush the water heater and test smoke detectors”), keeps a log of repairs with photos and contractor info, and helps troubleshoot: “My dishwasher is making a grinding noise” gets a diagnostic walkthrough before you call a repair service.

Watch out for
  • Home details (make/model of appliances, contractor contacts) are useful for social engineering
Stay safe
  • Keep home data processed locally or through a trusted provider
  • Don’t store alarm codes or safe combinations in the agent’s memory

14 Photo & Memory Organization

MEDIUM RISK 3–5 hrs/month saved

Point it at your 47,000-photo library and it starts making sense of the chaos. It identifies duplicates, groups by events (“Thanksgiving 2025,” “Yosemite Trip”), and tags people and places. Later, ask “find photos of Mom at the beach” or “show me pictures from our first apartment” and get instant results. It can also create highlight reels, select the best shots from a burst, and generate photo books with captions.

Watch out for
  • Photos contain EXIF data (location, timestamps) and biometric information (faces)
  • Sending photos to cloud AI means uploading your personal memories to a third party
Stay safe
  • Process photos locally — this is a great use case for local AI models
  • Strip EXIF data before any cloud processing

15 Journaling & Reflection

MEDIUM RISK Wellness benefit, 15–30 min/day

Text your thoughts to OpenClaw throughout the day — no special app needed, just your messaging app. It compiles them into a structured journal entry each evening, picks up on mood patterns over time (“You tend to feel more stressed on Sundays — might be anticipating the week ahead”), and can prompt reflection questions. Months later, ask “what was I thinking about in January?” and get a thoughtful summary.

Watch out for
  • Journal entries are deeply personal — the most sensitive data you could hand an AI
  • Mood and mental health data is a special category under privacy laws
Stay safe
  • Use a local AI model — your innermost thoughts should never leave your machine
  • Encrypt stored journal data at rest
  • Review the agent’s memory files periodically to ensure nothing unexpected is stored

16 Shopping & Deal Finding

LOW-MEDIUM RISK 2–4 hrs/week saved

“I need a new espresso machine under $500. I care about build quality and milk steaming, don’t care about smart features.” OpenClaw researches options, compares prices across retailers, reads reviews (filtering out fake ones), and monitors for price drops. It can track items on your wishlist and alert you when something goes on sale: “Those boots you liked are 30% off at Nordstrom today.”

Watch out for
  • Web browsing exposes the agent to prompt injection via malicious product pages
  • Recommendations could be biased or hallucinated
Stay safe
  • Never give the agent your credit card or purchasing credentials
  • Verify prices and availability yourself before buying
  • Use a sandboxed browser

17 Podcast & Reading Curation

LOW RISK 2–3 hrs/week saved

Drowning in podcast subscriptions and a 200-article reading list? OpenClaw triages it for you. It listens to (or reads transcripts of) new episodes and articles, then sends a daily digest: “Three things worth your time today” with short summaries. If something matches your current interests, it flags it as priority. It learns your taste over time — less of what you skip, more of what you engage with.

Watch out for
  • Your reading habits reveal a lot about your interests, beliefs, and concerns
Stay safe
  • Use read-only access to your podcast and reading apps
  • Process locally if you’re uncomfortable with interest profiling

18 Pet Care Management

LOW RISK 1–2 hrs/week saved

Tracks vet appointments, medication schedules, vaccination records, and feeding routines for all your pets. “When is Biscuit’s next heartworm pill?” — instant answer. It sends reminders for flea treatments, flags when it’s time to reorder food based on your buying pattern, and can log symptoms if your pet is unwell (“Biscuit threw up twice today, ate less than usual”) to share with the vet later.

Watch out for
  • Minimal risk — pet data isn’t particularly sensitive
  • Don’t rely on it for veterinary diagnosis
Stay safe
  • Treat health symptoms as notes for your vet, not as diagnoses

19 Hobby & Side Project Tracker

LOW RISK 1–2 hrs/week saved

Whether you’re restoring a vintage motorcycle, learning woodworking, growing a garden, or building a drone, OpenClaw keeps track of your projects. It logs where you left off (“Last session: sanded the tabletop, need to apply first coat of polyurethane”), tracks parts and materials, stores reference links and tutorials, and reminds you of seasonal tasks (“Time to start tomato seedlings indoors”). It’s like a personal wiki for everything you’re working on.

Watch out for
  • Minimal risk — hobby data is low-sensitivity
Stay safe
  • Standard precautions are sufficient for this use case

20 Meeting & Conversation Notes

MEDIUM RISK 30–60 min/meeting saved

Record a conversation (with everyone’s permission), and OpenClaw transcribes it, extracts the key points, and sends you a clean summary with action items. Great for doctor’s appointments (“take 200mg twice daily, follow up in 6 weeks”), parent-teacher conferences, or any situation where you’re too focused on the conversation to take good notes.

Watch out for
  • Recordings contain sensitive personal conversations
  • Legal requirements for consent vary by jurisdiction (some require all-party consent)
  • Action items could be mis-attributed or hallucinated
Stay safe
  • Use local transcription (Whisper) before sending text to any AI
  • Always get consent from everyone being recorded
  • Review action items and summaries before acting on them

Setting It Up Securely

Don’t let the length of this section scare you. We’ll walk through everything step by step. If you’ve ever set up a Wi-Fi router or installed an app from the command line, you can do this.

How OpenClaw Works Under the Hood

Before you install, it helps to understand what’s actually happening. Here’s the simple version:

OpenClaw is a program that runs in the background on a computer, kind of like how Spotify runs in the background playing music. But instead of playing songs, it’s listening for your messages and doing tasks.

It has three main parts:

  • The Gateway — Think of this as the “front door” of OpenClaw. It’s what receives your messages from Telegram, Signal, etc. Just like a real front door, you want it locked and only accessible to you.
  • The AI connection — When you send a message, OpenClaw forwards it to an AI (like Claude or ChatGPT) to figure out what to do. This part happens over the internet, which means your messages travel to the AI company’s servers. (Unless you use a local AI model — more on that later.)
  • Skills — These are like apps within OpenClaw. Each skill teaches it how to do something specific: one skill connects to your email, another to your smart home, another to your calendar. You install only the ones you need.

OpenClaw also has a memory. It remembers things between conversations so you don’t have to repeat yourself. This is stored in simple text files on your computer called SOUL.md and MEMORY.md.

Where Should You Run It?

You need a computer that stays on all the time (or at least when you want to use OpenClaw). You have a few options, from most secure to most convenient:

Option A: A Small Cloud Server (Recommended)

This is like renting a tiny computer in a data center. Services like DigitalOcean or Hetzner offer these for about $5–10 per month. The advantage: it’s completely separate from your personal computer, so even if something goes wrong, your laptop and personal files aren’t affected. Think of it like having a P.O. box instead of giving out your home address.

Option B: An Old Computer or Raspberry Pi

Got an old laptop collecting dust? A Raspberry Pi ($60)? Perfect. Dedicate it to OpenClaw. Plug it in, connect it to your Wi-Fi, and let it run. The key: don’t use the same computer you do your banking and personal stuff on.

Option C: Your Own Computer (Least Secure)

You can run it on your everyday laptop or desktop, but it’s like inviting a stranger to share your desk. If something goes wrong, they have access to everything. If you go this route, at minimum run OpenClaw inside a virtual machine (a “computer within your computer”) to keep it separated.

Installing OpenClaw Step by Step

You’ll need to open a terminal (also called command line or command prompt). On Mac, search for “Terminal” in Spotlight. On Windows, search for “PowerShell.” On Linux, you probably already know where it is.

  1. Create a separate user account for OpenClaw. This is like creating a guest account on your computer — it limits what OpenClaw can access. Type these commands one at a time, pressing Enter after each: 
    sudo adduser openclaw --disabled-password
sudo su - openclaw

    (The computer will ask for your password for the first command — this is your computer’s admin password.)

  2. Download the installer. This fetches the official install script and saves it as a file so you can look at it first: 
    curl -fsSL https://get.openclaw.ai -o install.sh
  3. Run the installer: 
    bash install.sh

    This will take a minute or two. It downloads OpenClaw and sets everything up.

  4. Run the setup wizard. OpenClaw will walk you through connecting your messaging app, choosing your AI provider, and setting a security password (called a “gateway token”). Just follow the prompts — it’s like setting up a new phone.

  5. Run the security check. Think of this like running a virus scan right after installing new software: 
    openclaw security audit --fix

    This automatically fixes common security issues. Run this command regularly — ideally once a week.

Keeping the Front Door Locked

Remember the Gateway — OpenClaw’s “front door”? By default, it might be open to anyone on the internet. That’s like leaving your front door unlocked in a busy city. Here’s how to lock it:

The most important thing: Make sure OpenClaw only listens for connections from your own computer, not the whole internet. Add this line to your OpenClaw settings:

OPENCLAW_GATEWAY_HOST=127.0.0.1

In plain English, this tells OpenClaw: “Only accept messages from this computer, ignore everyone else.”

If you’re running OpenClaw on a separate server and need to reach it from your phone/laptop, use a tool called Tailscale (free for personal use). It creates a private, encrypted tunnel between your devices — like a secret passageway that only you know about. Much safer than opening OpenClaw to the internet.

Turn off network broadcasting. By default, OpenClaw announces itself to other devices on your Wi-Fi (like how AirDrop shows nearby devices). Turn this off:

export OPENCLAW_DISABLE_BONJOUR=1

Passwords and Access

When OpenClaw connects to your email, calendar, or other services, it needs passwords or “tokens” (special passwords for apps). Here are the golden rules:

  • Give OpenClaw the least access possible. If it only needs to read your email, don’t give it permission to send or delete. Most services let you create limited-access tokens — look for options labeled “read-only” or “limited permissions.”
  • Create separate passwords for OpenClaw. Don’t reuse your personal login. If OpenClaw is compromised, you only need to change one password, not all of them.
  • Never type passwords directly into OpenClaw’s chat. Use the settings/configuration file instead. The chat history is less secure.

Installing Skills (Add-ons) Safely

Skills are how you teach OpenClaw new tricks — there are skills for email, smart home, fitness trackers, and thousands more on ClawHub (OpenClaw’s app store).

Be very careful what you install In early 2026, researchers found that roughly 1 in 5 skills on ClawHub contained hidden malicious code — designed to steal your data or take control of your system. This is the single biggest risk for most OpenClaw users.

Think of it like installing apps on your phone in the early days before app stores had good reviews and verification. To stay safe:

  • Only install skills from well-known, verified authors — look for the verified badge on ClawHub
  • Stick to popular, well-reviewed skills with lots of users and recent updates
  • Be extra suspicious of skills that ask for system/admin access — most legitimate skills don’t need it
  • Don’t let skills auto-update. A safe skill today could push a malicious update tomorrow. Update manually after checking what changed.
  • After installing any new skill, run the security check: openclaw security audit --fix

Managing AI Costs

OpenClaw itself is free, but the AI it uses is not. Every time you send a message, OpenClaw forwards it to an AI service (Claude, GPT, etc.) and you pay a small amount. Usually pennies per message, but it adds up — especially if the agent is doing automated tasks all day.

  • Set a spending cap. Every AI provider lets you set a monthly limit. Start at $20–50/month and adjust as you learn your usage patterns.
  • Create a separate API key just for OpenClaw. An API key is like a special password that lets OpenClaw use the AI service. Keep it separate from any other AI accounts you have. If something goes wrong, you can disable just that key.
  • Watch for unusual spikes. If your bill suddenly jumps, it could mean something is wrong — like the agent getting stuck in a loop or, worse, someone misusing your setup.

Keeping It Up to Date

OpenClaw has had 60+ security issues reported in its first 4 months Some of these were critical — meaning an attacker could take over your system remotely. Keeping OpenClaw updated is not optional, it’s essential. Think of it like locking your car: you just have to do it every time.

Make a habit of doing these things regularly:

  • Every week: Run openclaw security audit --fix to catch and fix any new issues
  • When updates are available: Install them promptly. OpenClaw will usually tell you when a new version is ready.
  • Every month: Open the SOUL.md and MEMORY.md files on your computer and skim through them. If you see anything that looks like instructions you didn’t write, or strange text you don’t recognize, that could be a sign of tampering.

Security Principles & Verification

10 Rules to Live By

  1. Start small: Begin with low-risk use cases (meal planning, travel, hobbies) and build confidence
  2. Least privilege: Every integration gets the minimum permissions needed
  3. Human in the loop: Require approval for all actions that change something — especially at first
  4. Defense in depth: Container + firewall + auth + scoped tokens + monitoring
  5. Assume compromise: Design your setup so a hijacked agent has limited blast radius
  6. Local AI for sensitive data: Use Ollama + Llama/Mistral for financial, health, or personal data
  7. Regular audits: Run openclaw security audit weekly, review memory files monthly
  8. Stay updated: 60+ CVEs in 4 months — always run the latest version
  9. Vet all skills: Never install unaudited third-party skills
  10. Monitor costs: Set API spending limits to detect abuse early

Verification Checklist

After setup, verify security by:

  1. Running openclaw security audit --fix — confirm zero findings
  2. Port scanning your host — confirm 18789 is not externally accessible
  3. Testing that the gateway rejects unauthenticated WebSocket connections
  4. Verifying API key spending limits are set
  5. Confirming mDNS is disabled (OPENCLAW_DISABLE_BONJOUR=1)
  6. Testing a benign prompt injection to check guardrails
  7. Reviewing SOUL.md and MEMORY.md for unexpected content
  8. Verifying allow_url_actions: false in openclaw.yaml
  9. Confirming version 2026.3.12+ (patches all known CVEs)
  10. Testing that non-localhost WebSocket connections are rejected
  11. Verifying Docker runs as non-root with --cap-drop=ALL and --read-only

Pentester / Whitehat Review

This section presents findings from security researchers who’ve tested OpenClaw in the real world. It’s more technical, aimed at people who want to understand the deeper threat landscape.

Critical Attack Vectors

1. The “Lethal Trifecta” (Penligent/OctoClaw research)

The combination of System Access + Execution Power + Untrusted Ingestion creates a perfect storm. Most OpenClaw setups have all three by default. Never combine all three in a single agent instance.

2. Time-Shifted Memory Poisoning (Palo Alto Networks)

Malicious inputs written to SOUL.md/MEMORY.md can appear benign at ingestion but “detonate” later — logic bomb-style. Palo Alto mapped OpenClaw to every category in the OWASP Top 10 for Agentic Applications.

Mitigation: Treat memory files as code, not data. Use file integrity monitoring, enforce read-only permissions during runtime.

3. Log Poisoning → Indirect Prompt Injection

Attackers write malicious content to log files via WebSocket requests. Since the agent reads its own logs, this becomes an injection vector. Mitigation: Make logs write-only from the agent’s perspective.

4. Container Escape via API

CVE-2026-25253’s WebSocket hijacking works even inside Docker containers — the container boundary doesn’t block it.

5. Localhost Trust Assumption (ClawJacked)

OpenClaw’s gateway exempted localhost from rate limiting entirely. Browser JavaScript could brute-force the gateway at hundreds of guesses/second with no lockout and no logging.

Docker Hardening

# docker-compose.yml — hardened deployment
version: '3.8'
services:
  openclaw:
    image: openclaw/openclaw:2026.3.12  # Pin to patched version
    read_only: true                      # Read-only root filesystem
    cap_drop:
      - ALL                              # Drop all Linux capabilities
    security_opt:
      - no-new-privileges:true           # Prevent privilege escalation
      - seccomp=openclaw-seccomp.json    # Custom seccomp profile
    user: "1000:1000"                    # Non-root user
    ports:
      - "127.0.0.1:18789:18789"         # Bind to loopback ONLY
    volumes:
      - ./config:/app/config:ro          # Config read-only
      - ./data:/app/data                 # Data writable (minimal)
    mem_limit: 2g
    cpus: 1.0
    networks:
      - openclaw-internal

  egress-proxy:
    image: nginx:alpine
    networks:
      - openclaw-internal
      - external

networks:
  openclaw-internal:
    internal: true   # No direct internet access for OpenClaw
  external:
    driver: bridge

Key points:

  • internal: true network forces all traffic through an egress proxy with an allowlist
  • Even a fully compromised agent cannot exfiltrate data to arbitrary servers
  • Never mount docker.sock — it grants container escape
  • Never use --network=host — it removes network isolation

Incident Response Plan

If you suspect compromise:

  1. Immediately: Disconnect OpenClaw from the network
  2. Within 1 hour: Rotate ALL credentials the agent had access to
  3. Audit: Check logs for unexpected system.run calls
  4. Review: Examine SOUL.md/MEMORY.md for injected instructions
  5. Scan: Check for backdoor skills or modified skill files
  6. Report: File a GitHub Security Advisory if you find exploitation evidence

OpenClaw vs Claude Code

Anthropic has been building features that, piece by piece, cover much of what OpenClaw does — but within a managed, secure, Claude-only ecosystem. Here’s how all the pieces fit together.

The Claude Ecosystem at a Glance

Anthropic now has three main products that overlap with OpenClaw in different ways:

  • Claude Code — A command-line tool for developers. Lives in your terminal or code editor. Reads and writes code, runs commands, manages git.
  • Claude Cowork — A desktop app for everyone else. Lives on your Mac or PC. Handles documents, files, spreadsheets, research — knowledge work that doesn’t require coding.
  • Claude Channels — A plugin system that lets you talk to Claude Code through messaging apps like Telegram and Discord — the most direct competitor to OpenClaw’s core concept.

Channels: Anthropic’s Answer to OpenClaw’s Chat Model

This is the feature that most directly competes with OpenClaw. Launched March 20, 2026 as a research preview.

How it works: You install a messaging plugin (currently Telegram, Discord, or iMessage), connect it to your Claude Code session, and then you can text Claude from your phone just like you’d text OpenClaw. Claude receives your message, does work on your computer using its full environment (your files, code, tools), and replies back in the same chat.

The key difference from OpenClaw: When a message arrives through Channels, Claude already has deep context about your project — it can see your files, your git history, your installed tools. OpenClaw’s messaging is its native interface — it was built messaging-first — but it operates as a separate layer making API calls without that deep local environment integration.

Think of it this way: OpenClaw is like texting a personal assistant who has keys to many rooms in your house. Channels is like texting a specialist who’s already sitting at your desk with all your files open. The specialist knows more about what’s in front of them, but the assistant can go to more places.

Where OpenClaw still wins:

  • Platform coverage — OpenClaw supports Signal, WhatsApp, Slack, Teams, Telegram, Discord, and iMessage. Channels currently only supports Telegram, Discord, and iMessage.
  • Always on — OpenClaw runs as a background service, always ready. Channels requires an active Claude Code session to be running on your computer.
  • Any AI model — OpenClaw works with Claude, GPT, local models, anything. Channels is Claude-only.
  • Breadth of integrations — OpenClaw has 50+ service integrations (smart home, email, calendar, fitness). Channels inherits whatever tools Claude Code has access to, which is powerful for dev work but doesn’t natively connect to your Nest thermostat.

Where Channels wins:

  • Security — Sender allowlists with pairing codes. Enterprise admins can control which plugins are allowed. OpenClaw has nothing comparable.
  • Deep environment integration — Full access to your project files, git, and development tools. OpenClaw treats your computer as one of many services it connects to; Claude Code treats it as home base.
  • No CVE anxiety — Managed by Anthropic’s security team.

Cowork: The Desktop Agent for Non-Coders

If Claude Code is for developers, Cowork is for everyone else. It’s a desktop app (runs inside the Claude Desktop app) where you give Claude access to a folder on your computer and tell it what to do.

What it can do: You hand it a pile of documents and say “organize these into a summary report” or “extract all the key dates from these contracts into a spreadsheet.” It reads files, creates new ones, and even interacts with other apps on your screen (browsers, Excel, dashboards) to complete multi-step tasks. You come back to a finished deliverable.

How it compares to OpenClaw: Both aim to be “AI that does things, not just answers questions.” But they live in completely different worlds:

  • OpenClaw automates your life — email, calendar, smart home, meals, fitness, travel. You interact through messaging apps throughout your day.
  • Cowork automates your desk work — documents, files, research, data. You interact through the desktop app when you’re at your computer.

They don’t really compete — they serve different moments in your day.

Dispatch: Text Your Computer from Anywhere

Dispatch is a feature built into Cowork that lets you text Claude from the Claude mobile app and have it do things on your computer while you’re away. “Pull together the notes from this week’s meetings into a summary doc” — sent from your phone at lunch, finished by the time you get back to your desk.

How it compares to OpenClaw’s messaging: On the surface, Dispatch looks similar — you send a text, AI does work. But the differences matter:

  • OpenClaw’s messaging is universal — it works through Signal, Telegram, WhatsApp, any app you already use. Dispatch only works through the Claude mobile app.
  • OpenClaw is always on — it runs as a service, always listening. Dispatch requires your Mac/PC to be awake with the Cowork app open.
  • OpenClaw connects to your life — email, calendar, smart home, fitness. Dispatch works with files and apps on your desktop.
  • Dispatch maintains a single conversation — Claude remembers what you talked about earlier. OpenClaw also has persistent memory (SOUL.md/MEMORY.md), but it’s more like long-term memory than a continuous chat thread.

Anthropic also offers scheduled tasks — you can set up recurring automations (“every morning at 8am, summarize my unread emails”). These can run on Anthropic’s cloud servers even when your computer is off, which is something OpenClaw can’t do without your own hardware being online. But OpenClaw’s scheduling is broader — it can automate anything it has a skill for, not just desktop tasks.

Agent Teams: Multiple AIs Working Together

This is the most futuristic feature — and the one with the least OpenClaw overlap. Agent Teams lets you spin up multiple Claude Code instances that work on different parts of a problem simultaneously, communicate with each other, and coordinate through a shared task list. One might review code for security issues while another checks performance while a third writes tests.

OpenClaw is fundamentally a single-agent system. It has a rich skills ecosystem that extends what one agent can do, but it can’t coordinate multiple AI instances in parallel. Agent Teams solves a different problem entirely — complex technical work that benefits from parallel exploration.

When to Use Which

Use Claude Code + Channels if you’re a developer who wants to text your dev environment from your phone, with strong security and no CVEs to worry about.

Use Claude Cowork + Dispatch if you want a desktop assistant for documents, files, and research tasks — especially if you’re not technical.

Use OpenClaw if you want to automate your whole life — email, calendar, smart home, health, meals, and dozens of other services — through messaging apps you already use, with the freedom to choose any AI model and keep your data on your own hardware.

Many people use both Claude’s ecosystem for coding and desk work, OpenClaw for everything else. They serve different moments in your day. As Channels adds more platforms and OpenClaw matures its security, the overlap will grow — but for now, the sweet spot is using each for what it does best.

NemoClaw: OpenClaw with a Security Cage

If you’ve been reading this guide thinking “OpenClaw sounds amazing but the security stuff scares me,” NVIDIA built something specifically for you. It’s called NemoClaw, announced at GTC 2026 on March 16 by Jensen Huang. It’s free, open-source, and currently in alpha (very early) status.

What Is NemoClaw, Exactly?

NemoClaw is not a replacement for OpenClaw. It’s a security wrapper around OpenClaw. Think of it this way:

OpenClaw is the employee. NemoClaw is the building with locked doors, security cameras, and badge readers.

When you install NemoClaw, it takes OpenClaw and puts it inside a secure “sandbox” — an isolated environment on your computer where OpenClaw can do its work but can’t wander into areas it shouldn’t. It’s still OpenClaw doing the actual work. NemoClaw just makes sure it stays in its lane.

Here’s what that means in practice:

  • OpenClaw can’t access your files outside of its designated folder. Your photos, documents, and personal files are off-limits unless you explicitly allow it.
  • OpenClaw can’t talk to the internet freely. By default, all internet access is blocked. You have to specifically approve each service it can reach — “yes, you can talk to Telegram” or “yes, you can reach the Claude API.” Everything else is denied.
  • Your passwords never enter the sandbox. When OpenClaw needs to log into a service, the credentials are stored on the secure “outside” and injected at the point of use. Even a fully compromised agent can’t steal them.
  • Every action gets checked against your rules. Before OpenClaw actually does something, NemoClaw checks: “Is this action allowed by the policies the user set up?” If not, it blocks it.

Is NemoClaw Actually Safer?

Significantly, yes. That was the whole point of building it. Here’s why:

Remember those scary security issues from earlier in this guide? The 60+ CVEs, the malicious skills, the exposed instances? NemoClaw addresses them at a fundamental level:

ProblemOpenClawNemoClaw
Malicious skill steals your dataSkill has full internet access; data goes to the attacker’s serverAll internet blocked except your approved list. Skill can’t phone home.
Agent reads your personal filesFull access to everything on the computer by defaultCan only access its own sandbox folder. Your files are invisible to it.
Passwords get exposedStored in config files the agent can readStored outside the sandbox. Agent never sees them.
Sensitive data sent to cloud AIEverything goes to whichever AI you chosePrivacy router detects personal info and redirects to a local AI model instead.

That last point is especially cool: NemoClaw includes a privacy router. When you send a message, it checks whether it contains personal information. If it does, the query gets automatically sent to a local AI model running on your own hardware — it never leaves your computer. Non-sensitive queries can go to faster cloud AI. You get the best of both worlds without having to think about it.

NemoClaw is brand new It was released March 16, 2026 — less than two weeks old at the time of writing. It’s alpha software with rough edges. Users report hours of troubleshooting during setup, and NVIDIA explicitly warns it’s not production-ready. It’s the right direction, but expect bumps.

Installing NemoClaw

NemoClaw is harder to install than plain OpenClaw because it has more moving parts (the sandbox, the inference router, the policy engine). But the process is mostly automated.

What You Need

  • A computer with at least 8 GB of RAM (16 GB recommended) and 20 GB of free disk space
  • Mac (Apple Silicon — M1/M2/M3/M4), Linux (Ubuntu 22.04+), or Windows (via WSL). Intel Macs are not supported.
  • Docker installed and running — this is the software that creates the secure sandbox.
    • On Mac: Install Docker Desktop (free for personal use) and make sure it’s running (look for the whale icon in your menu bar)
    • On Linux: Docker is usually pre-installed or one command away (sudo apt install docker.io)
    • On Windows: Install Docker Desktop with WSL backend

Step by Step

  1. Open a terminal (on Mac, search for “Terminal” in Spotlight; on Windows, open PowerShell or WSL).
  2. Run the installer. Copy and paste this single line: 
    curl -fsSL https://www.nvidia.com/nemoclaw.sh | bash

    This downloads and runs NVIDIA’s setup script. It will take a few minutes — it’s downloading the sandbox image (~2.4 GB) and setting things up.

  3. Follow the setup wizard. It will ask you:
    • Which AI model provider to use (Claude, GPT, NVIDIA’s own models, or a local model)
    • Your API key for that provider
    • Which messaging app to connect (Telegram, Discord, etc.)

    Just follow the prompts — the wizard walks you through each step.

  4. Connect to your agent: 
    nemoclaw my-assistant connect

    This opens the OpenClaw interface running inside its secure sandbox.

  5. Start chatting. Open Telegram or Discord on your phone and send a message to your bot. NemoClaw is running OpenClaw behind the scenes, but now with all the security guardrails in place.
Expect some troubleshooting NemoClaw is alpha software. Common gotchas: Docker not running (start Docker Desktop first), not enough RAM (you need 8 GB minimum), and network policies blocking services you need (you may need to edit a config file to allow specific websites). The GitHub Issues page is active and helpful.

Should You Use OpenClaw or NemoClaw?

Use NemoClaw if:

  • Security is your top concern — you want deny-by-default protection
  • You handle sensitive data (health, financial, personal) and want the privacy router
  • You have NVIDIA hardware and want optimized local AI inference
  • You’re comfortable with a rougher setup experience in exchange for better security
  • You want to use OpenClaw skills but don’t trust ClawHub’s safety track record

Use plain OpenClaw if:

  • You want the simplest setup experience
  • You’re on an Intel Mac (NemoClaw doesn’t support it)
  • You’re experimenting and learning — the lower complexity helps
  • You don’t want the overhead of Docker and containers
  • You want mature, more battle-tested software (NemoClaw is weeks old)

The bottom line: If OpenClaw is a powerful sports car, NemoClaw adds roll cages, airbags, and a speed limiter. It’s the same car, but designed so that when things go wrong — and with software this complex, they eventually will — the damage is contained. As it matures past alpha, NemoClaw will likely become the recommended way to run OpenClaw for anyone who cares about security.

Steinberger’s Perspective

Based on Peter Steinberger’s public statements — his blog (Feb 15, 2026), Pragmatic Engineer podcast, Lex Fridman #491, Fortune/TechCrunch/CNBC interviews, and GitHub activity.

His Philosophy: Empowerment, Not Containment

Steinberger positions OpenClaw as “an AI that actually does things” — a tool for people who want to reclaim their time. His vision is building “an agent that even my mum can use.” He frames AI agents as a new skill: “You pick up the guitar — you’re not going to be good at the guitar in the first day.” He calls this “agentic engineering” and explicitly distinguishes it from “vibe coding.”

Where Steinberger Would Push Back on This Guide

  1. Absolutist “NEVER” prohibitions: He favors graduated trust models over blanket bans. Instead of “NEVER connect door locks,” he’d say “start read-only, level up as you validate.”
  2. Model interchangeability: A core design principle — you can route sensitive tasks to local models and mundane tasks to cloud APIs on the same agent. This guide could emphasize that more.
  3. The empowerment narrative: He’d want people excited first, cautious second. Fair point — but this guide errs on the side of safety.

Where He Acknowledges the Risks

  • Announced moving OpenClaw to an independent open-source foundation (Apache/PSF model) on Feb 14, 2026
  • Active in GitHub Security Advisory work, though critical of GitHub’s reporting system

Uncomfortable Truths

  1. ClawHavoc response was slow: Steinberger was criticized for saying he was “too busy” to address malicious skills. C2 infrastructure stayed operational for days.
  2. Community as security asset: ClawCon drew 1,000+ attendees; Cisco’s DefenseClaw tool and VirusTotal scanning came from community contributions.
  3. One maintainer’s candid warning: “If you can’t understand how to run a command line, this is far too dangerous of a project for you to use safely.”

The Balanced Take

This guide errs on the side of security researchers (Microsoft, Palo Alto Networks, Cisco) rather than the creator’s more optimistic view. That’s the right call for a guide focused on keeping you safe. But Steinberger’s graduated-trust approach (“start minimal, expand carefully”) is valid for people who understand the risks they’re accepting.
